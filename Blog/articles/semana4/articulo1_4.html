Author: Jose Miguel Garcia · Source: DW News

The Shortage of Apprentices in Germany

The article “Apprentices wanted, Germany lacks young talent” by Sabine Kinkartz (DW, August 28, 2025) addresses the growing scarcity of young people willing and prepared to participate in Germany’s dual vocational training system. This model, internationally recognized for combining practical instruction in companies with theoretical education in vocational schools, now faces an imbalance that threatens its sustainability.

The problem is evident in the figures: in 2024 there were around 519,000 training positions available but only 432,000 applicants, a situation that contrasts sharply with past decades when demand exceeded supply. Furthermore, while the participation of German applicants continues to decline, the number of foreign apprentices—mainly from Vietnam, Syria, and Ukraine—has doubled over the past decade. This trend highlights both the importance of immigration and the diminishing interest or preparedness of the local youth population.

Beyond demographic decline, one of the main obstacles identified by companies is the lack of basic competencies among domestic applicants. According to the German Chamber of Industry and Commerce, 44% of companies reported deficiencies in German language skills, while a similar percentage pointed to gaps in elementary mathematics. These shortcomings not only hinder academic performance during training but also jeopardize the long-term employability of young people.

In response, companies have begun to offer increasingly attractive incentives to attract apprentices, such as extended vacation time, bonuses, and flexible working conditions. Nevertheless, such measures prove insufficient when the core problem lies in inadequate academic preparation and a lack of commitment to professional training.

In sum, the article demonstrates that the current shortage of apprentices in Germany is not merely a temporary issue of unfilled vacancies, but rather a structural challenge that involves the education system, the labor market, and the motivation of young people. While immigration partly alleviates the shortage, the long-term sustainability of the dual training model will depend on strengthening basic skills and fostering greater interest among German youth in technical and vocational opportunities.
