An Invitation to Understand Mycelium at the Materialidades Conjuntas Openlab

Author: Jose Miguel Garcia · Source: Idartes

“Working with mycelium means recognizing that not everything in nature must serve human purposes. We wanted to relate to fungi with care and sensitivity, not just utility,” says Natalia Cossio, biologist, ecologist, and member of the collective Habitar el Micelio. She is one of the coordinators of Materialidades Conjuntas, a project that won the 2024 Plataforma Bogotá Grant in Art, Science, and Technology, awarded by the District Institute of the Arts (Idartes).

Cossio, along with visual artist Isabel Izquierdo and architect and professor Carlos Acosta, led this space where, over several weeks, artists, designers, and applied science experts came together to explore the living possibilities of mycelium and open questions about how humans relate to other species.

Beyond extraction: a cooperative vision

The starting point was the concept of biomaterials, which guided a collaborative methodology with fungi. Instead of extractivism, biomaterials promote a cooperative system based on cultivation and reuse. This perspective moves away from an anthropocentric view of consumption. According to Cossio, these lessons go beyond biology: “Fungi, through their way of living, the collaborations they weave, and the way they grow, also teach us to decentralize the human perspective in how we see and perceive life.”

The laboratory combined theory with experimentation, cultivation, play, and dialogue. Participants engaged in key encounters, including a talk with researcher Jaret Jiménez, ecodesigner and founder of ITESO’s materioteca, who expanded the systemic understanding of materiality; and a visit to Kuiica, a lab dedicated to transforming plastics and waste, where participants critically analyzed the ecological impact of biomaterial production.
